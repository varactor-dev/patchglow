1. Welcome

PatchGlow is a visual modular synthesizer that runs in your browser. It teaches you synthesis by showing you what's happening — every waveform, every filter sweep, every envelope shape is rendered in real time on each module's display. No experience required. If you can drag a cable and turn a knob, you can make sounds.

2. Getting Started

  • Start Audio — Click the glowing START AUDIO button. Browsers require a user gesture before they'll play sound — this is that gesture.
  • Add Modules — Click the ADD buttons in the toolbar (OSCILLATOR, FILTER, ENVELOPE, etc.). Each module appears in the rack.
  • Connect Cables — Click and drag from an output port (OUT) to an input port (IN). A preview cable follows your cursor. Release on a compatible input to complete the connection.
  • Remove Cables — Click a cable to select it (it brightens). Press Delete or Backspace to remove it. Press Escape to deselect.
  • Remove Modules — Right-click a module and select "Remove." All connected cables are removed too.
  • Turn Knobs — Click and drag vertically (up = increase, down = decrease). Hold Shift for fine control (10x slower). Double-click to reset to default. Scroll wheel also works.
  • Play Notes — Use your computer keyboard — keys map to a piano octave.

Port Colors

Every port has a colored ring that tells you what kind of signal it carries:

Audio Amber ring — the actual sound waveform
CV (Control Voltage) Cyan ring — a signal that controls a parameter
Gate Magenta ring — an on/off trigger signal

3. Your First Patch

Follow these five steps to build a complete subtractive synth voice from scratch. By the end, you'll have a playable instrument with keyboard control, volume shaping, filtering, and modulation.

Step 1: Oscillator + Output

Add an Oscillator and an Output module. Connect Oscillator OUT → Output IN (drag from the amber OUT port to the amber IN port). You hear a continuous tone.

The oscillator's display shows the waveform in the top panel and the frequency spectrum below — try switching between sine, triangle, sawtooth, and square using the WAVE switch and watch both displays change dramatically.

Step 2: Add Keyboard

Add a Keyboard module. Connect Keyboard CV → Oscillator V/OCT (drag from the cyan CV port to the cyan V/OCT port). Now press keys A through K on your computer keyboard.

The pitch changes, but the sound never stops — that's because nothing is controlling when the sound plays. Right now, the oscillator just drones.

Step 3: Add Envelope + VCA

Add an Envelope and a VCA module. Rewire:

  • Remove the cable from Oscillator OUT → Output IN (click it, press Delete)
  • Connect Oscillator OUT → VCA IN (amber to amber)
  • Connect Envelope ENV OUT → VCA CV (cyan to cyan)
  • Connect VCA OUT → Output IN (amber to amber)
  • Connect Keyboard GATE → Envelope GATE (magenta to magenta)

Now press a key — the sound fades in and out! The envelope controls the VCA, which controls the volume. The envelope's display shows the ADSR curve with a glowing dot tracing along it as you play. Try adjusting Attack (how fast the sound appears) and Release (how long it fades after you let go).

Step 4: Add Filter

Add a Filter module between the Oscillator and VCA:

  • Remove the cable from Oscillator OUT → VCA IN
  • Connect Oscillator OUT → Filter IN (amber to amber)
  • Connect Filter OUT → VCA IN (amber to amber)

Sweep the CUTOFF knob and watch the filter's frequency response curve change in real time. Turn up RES (resonance) and hear the filter start to ring at the cutoff frequency. Try switching between lowpass, highpass, and bandpass filter types.

Step 5: Add LFO

Add an LFO module. Connect LFO OUT → Filter FREQ CV (cyan to cyan). The filter cutoff now moves on its own — you can see the cutoff line sweeping back and forth in the filter's display.

You've created modulation: one module automatically controlling another. Try adjusting the LFO RATE to change the speed, and DEPTH to change how far it sweeps.

5. Signal Types Explained

Three types of signals flow through PatchGlow, each with its own color:

Audio (Amber #ff6b35) — The actual sound waveform. This is what you hear — a rapidly vibrating signal (typically 20–20,000 cycles per second). Audio cables pulse with fast-moving particles.

CV / Control Voltage (Cyan #00e5ff) — A slow-moving signal that controls parameters. Think of it as a remote control — an LFO sending CV to a filter cutoff makes the cutoff knob turn automatically. CV cables pulse with slow, steady particles.

Gate (Magenta #ff2ecb) — An on/off signal, like a light switch. When you press a key, the gate goes high (on). When you release, it goes low (off). Gates trigger envelopes. Gate cables pulse at medium speed.

Cables are color-coded to match their signal type, and the animation speed differs: audio flows fast, CV flows slowly, gate pulses at a moderate pace.

6. Synthesis Concepts

What is a waveform?

Sound is vibration — air molecules pushing back and forth in a repeating pattern. The shape of that pattern is the waveform. In PatchGlow, you can see the waveform on the Oscillator and Output displays. Different shapes (sine, triangle, sawtooth, square) sound different because they contain different combinations of harmonics.

What are harmonics?

A pure tone (sine wave) has just one frequency. But most musical sounds are made of many frequencies stacked on top of each other — these are harmonics. The Oscillator's spectrum display (bottom panel) shows these as vertical bars. A sawtooth wave has many harmonics; a sine wave has just one.

What does a filter do?

A filter removes some frequencies and lets others pass through. A lowpass filter removes high frequencies (making the sound darker/warmer). A highpass filter removes low frequencies (making it thinner). Watch the Filter's frequency response curve to see exactly which frequencies pass through and which are blocked.

What is an envelope?

An envelope shapes how a value changes over time. The most common type is ADSR: Attack (rise), Decay (initial fall), Sustain (held level), Release (final fade). When you press a key, the envelope traces through these phases — watch the glowing dot on the Envelope display follow the curve in real time.

What is modulation?

Modulation is one signal controlling another. When you patch an LFO to a filter's cutoff, the LFO's slowly cycling waveform makes the cutoff move up and down automatically — creating a "wah-wah" or "wobble" effect. This is the core of synthesis: simple modules controlling each other to create complex, evolving sounds.

7. Saving & Loading

  • Autosave — PatchGlow automatically saves your work to your browser's local storage. If you close the tab and come back, your patch will be right where you left it.
  • Save — Click SAVE in the toolbar to download your patch as a .json file. You can share it or back it up.
  • Load — Click LOAD to import a previously saved .json patch file.
  • Reset — Click RESET to clear everything and start fresh. You'll be asked to confirm.
  • Demo — Click DEMO to load a reference patch — a complete subtractive synth voice with Keyboard → Oscillator → Filter → VCA → Output, plus an Envelope on the VCA and an LFO modulating the filter cutoff. It's a great starting point to explore.
